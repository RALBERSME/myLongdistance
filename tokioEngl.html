A glimpse into everyday life in Tokyo


Haru works as a kaishain (office worker) at a medium-sized company in the Shinjuku area. His day starts early: he gets up at 6:30, showers, and has breakfast (choushoku) with hieta ocha (cold tea) and onigiri (rice balls). Then he either takes the basu (bus) or the tetsudo (train) to work. The morning hours are often filled with emails and meetings.

The company places great emphasis on yoken (requirements) and no Ki (delivery deadlines); Haru checks KPIs (key performance indicators) and hinshitsu (quality) daily. During his lunch break, he goes with his colleagues to a kinjo no teishoku-ya (nearby set-meal restaurant) or to the konbini (convenience store) around the corner. There, he usually orders a simple bento box (Japanese lunchbox that transports various dishes in separate compartments so they don't mix), with tori no teriyaki (chicken in teriyaki style) or yakizakana (grilled fish), accompanied by ryokucha (green tea).

After work, Haru often plunges into the hustle and bustle of the commute home, whether to Shitamachi or modern districts like Shibuya or Otemachi, to meet friends. Sometimes he goes to the gym or takes a walk in Yoyogi Park to clear his head. In the evenings, he rarely cooks and prefers to prepare for working from home.

At home, he reads some news on his smartphone or listens to music from a podcast. Sleep is important, because tomorrow the daily routine with colleagues and new clients awaits him again.

Haru feels comfortable with the Japanese rhythm of life. Punctuality, harmony, and structure give him a sense of security.
