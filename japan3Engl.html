  • Tokio – ca. 14,5 million inhabitants (in the core city)



    Tokyo is the capital of Japan and one of the world's most important financial, political, and cultural centers. The metropolitan area encompasses over 37 million people, yet the core city offers a dense, modern skyline and diverse neighborhoods. Politically, Tokyo is home to the National Diet (Kokkai) and many ministries, while culturally, museums, theaters, and traditional temple complexes coexist. The city is a global transportation hub with several major airports, including Narita and Haneda. Economically, services, technology, media, and retail dominate. Tokyo offers a vibrant culinary scene, from sushi and ramen to innovative gastronomic concepts. For leisure, Akihabara, Shibuya, and Shinjuku attract visitors with shopping, nightlife, and trends. The growth momentum remains high due to urban infrastructure projects such as subway expansion and new construction projects.



  • Yokohama – ca. 3,9 million inhabitants



    Yokohama is Japan's second largest city and the gateway to the Kantō Plain. Its port is one of the largest in the world and shapes the region's economy, trade, and industry. The city offers a mix of modern skyscrapers and historical buildings, such as the Red Brick Warehouse District. Scientific and cultural institutions, including museums and theaters, enrich urban life. Commuters from the greater metropolitan area benefit from a well-developed rail and bus infrastructure.
    The Minato Mirai 21 district represents the city's new face with its financial, leisure, and convention centers. Yokohama places importance on creative neighborhoods, parks, and green spaces. The population is steadily growing, and the port remains a vital economic engine.



  • Osaka – ca. 2,8 million inhabitants



    Osaka is considered the culinary capital of Japan, famous for street food such as takoyaki and okonomiyaki. The city is a central economic and commercial hub in Kansai with a strong focus on industry, logistics, and services. Osaka is home to important cultural sites such as Osaka Castle and the vibrant Dotonbori district. In terms of transportation, a dense network of JR, Hankyu, and Hanshin lines connects the city with its surroundings. The Osaka International Exhibition Center is known for trade shows, concerts, and large conventions. The local Kansai dialect shapes the cultural identity. Osaka combines traditional craftsmanship with modern technology. The quality of life is defined by good food, friendly residents, and lively neighborhoods.



  • Nagoya – ca. 2,4 million inhabitants



    Nagoya is an industrial powerhouse in central Honshu and home to major companies such as Toyota. The city serves as a logistical hub with excellent connections to Shinkansen bullet train lines to Tokyo and Osaka. Culturally, Nagoya offers castles, museums, and the famous Atsuta Shrine. The economy benefits from the automotive, mechanical engineering, and electronics industries. The city center features shopping malls, modern office buildings, and vibrant nightlife. Nagoya University and research institutions drive innovation and education. Nagoya invests in infrastructure such as subway expansion and urban renewal. The city combines industrial strength with cultural diversity.



  • Sapporo – ca. 2 million inhabitants



    Sapporo is located on Hokkaido and is known for winter sports, beer culture, and the annual Snow Festival. The city offers a cool, clean quality of life with spacious parks and a friendly urban environment. The economy is based on food production, mechanical engineering, IT, and tourism, especially during the festival. Sapporo is a transportation hub with a good rail and road network, as well as a modern airport. The city offers a mix of historical districts, parks, and modern shopping areas. Seasonal festivals shape the cultural life and attract visitors from all over the region. Educational institutions and universities contribute to regional research. The area surrounding Sapporo offers ski resorts and nature reserves.

  • Kobe – ca. 1,5 million inhabitants



    Kobe is located in the Kansai region on the coast and is known for its international character. The city combines tradition with modern urban life, visible in architectural landmarks such as the Kobe Port Tower. Economically, trade, logistics, cutting-edge technologies, and tourism are dominant. Kobe is famous for Kobe beef, a diverse culinary scene, and charming neighborhoods. The infrastructure includes a well-developed public transportation system, port facilities, and industrial parks. Culturally, the city offers museums, castles, and a vibrant art scene. The surrounding area, including Kobe Shidami Park, provides recreational spaces close to the city center. Kobe strives for sustainable growth through innovation and internationalization.



  • Kyoto – ca. 1,4 million inhabitants



    For centuries, Kyoto was the cultural heart of Japan and remains so with its many shrines, temples, and gardens. The city is an oasis of traditional architecture alongside modern facilities. Economically, Kyoto focuses heavily on technology, research, education, and creative industries. Tourism shapes the urban landscape, especially in districts like Gion and Arashiyama. The infrastructure connects Kyoto well with Osaka, Nara, and the rest of the Kansai region via train lines. Culturally, Kyoto remains a center for art, music, and education, with universities and research institutions. The quality of life stems from quiet residential areas, green spaces, and a high-quality culinary scene. Kyoto preserves tradition while investing in new technologies.



  • Kawasaki – ca. 1,5 million inhabitants



    Located between Tokyo and Yokohama, Kawasaki is a major industrial and logistics hub. The city boasts a strong heavy industry sector as well as modern service industries. Economically, it is dominated by mechanical engineering, electronics, chemicals, and port logistics. Kawasaki seamlessly integrates industrial centers with residential areas and parks, particularly in the Tama area. Transportation links are excellent, with several JR lines, subway connections, and a major port. Culturally, Kawasaki offers museums, sports and leisure parks, and a growing program of cultural events. The city invests in research, education, and urban development projects to enhance quality of life. Kawasaki strives for balanced growth between industry and residential areas.



  • Saitama (Stadt) – ca. 1,36 million inhabitants



    Saitama is located north of Tokyo and is an important suburb with its own economic structure. The city benefits from a strong service and retail sector as well as from manufacturing industries. Transportation links are excellent, with Shinkansen connections to Tokyo and regional train lines. In terms of infrastructure, Saitama focuses on urban renewal, new residential areas, and green spaces. The city center offers shopping malls, cultural institutions, and sports facilities. Education and research are supported by local universities. The quality of life reflects a mixture of urban atmosphere and accessible surroundings. Saitama serves as an important part of the Tokyo metropolitan area with its own distinct identity.
