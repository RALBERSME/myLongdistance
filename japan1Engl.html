Why you should embark on this journey through Japan

First of all, there's the landscape, of course. Within a short distance, you'll find diverse landscapes: From the snow-capped peaks of the northern island of Hokkaido to the subtropical beaches of Okinawa – Japan offers everything in an impressively compact form.

It's a country that looks back on a long history and has developed a vibrant present from it. Temples, shrines, and kingdoms of the past meet avant-garde architecture, pop culture, and innovative technology. Tradition and modernity exist here in a palpable balance.

Food is considered an art form in Japan. From sushi, ramen, and kaiseki to regional specialties like Osaka-style okonomiyaki or Hiroshima-style katsu, many high-quality dishes await you.

But aesthetics aren't only found on the plate.
They are also significant in everyday life. Small details with a big impact: From perfectly run teahouses to tranquil Zen gardens
to modernly designed corridors in train stations.
Aesthetics accompany the travel route in a captivating way.



What are the essential cultural differences between Europeans and Japanese people? Japanese communication tends to be more indirect. Politeness, context, and nonverbal cues play a larger role. In Europe, direct statements are often common; misunderstandings are less likely to occur when using clear, direct language. In Japan, respect is shown in many situations through formal address, bowing, and appropriate levels of politeness. Europeans apply such hierarchies less strictly, sometimes using a more casual tone.

Japanese culture often emphasizes the well-being of the group, harmony, and social peace. In Europe, individuality is more strongly emphasized, even in public decisions or expressions of opinion. Consumption and daily rituals: In Japan, rituals such as properly pouring soy sauce on sushi or carefully placing chopsticks are typical. Europeans often experience a more direct, pragmatic approach to eating. Private vs. Public Space: Public spaces in Japan are often used more quietly; loudness on trains or in cafes is considered disruptive. In many European cities, social noise in public areas is more common. Gratification and gift-giving culture: Gratitude and appreciation can be expressed in the form of gifts, cards, or polite gestures – often in more formal settings than in Europe. Great importance is placed on cleanliness and punctuality.

Handling conflicts: Confrontations are generally avoided; conflicts are resolved through subtle signals or compromises. In many European cultures, open dialogue is more frequently sought.
